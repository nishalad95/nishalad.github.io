ML Projects & Articles

Can Twitter Predict the Peak of a Pandemic?

Nature Scientific Report September 2021: During the early stages of the COVID-19 pandemic, understanding the evolution of virus transmission before mass testing was made readily available is important for policy makers & government officials. This work involved analysing Twitter data to extract insights during the first wave of the pandemic. Statistical methods and NLP techniques were used to predict the occurence of an outbreak and analyse public opinions and attitudes towards government measures.

PhD Research

My research focuses on developing ML approaches tailored to optimising particle track reconstruction algorithms at the Large Hadron Collider experiment at CERN. The work involved leverages Graph Network architectures, Bayesian Inference and techniques in pattern recognition, in order to balance trade-off concerns between efficiency metrics and computing resources.

Connecting the Dots Conference

In 2022 I had the opportunity to present my work on Graph Network architectures and pattern recognition at the Connecting the Dots conference at the University of Princeton. The approach aims to iteratively extract compatible track candidates by leveraging Gaussian Mixture Reduction techniques, state vector extrapolation and embedding Kalman filters within the message passing mechanism. Pre-print article available here.

ML Predictor for Measurement-to-Track Association

Design and construction of a traditional ML classifier used to identify compatible hit-pairs of particle trajectories using geometrical input features. The classifier employs a Naive Bayes approach, with fitted kernel densities for the likelihood function. Using a fast lookup implementation, this approach was deployed into production software. Published article available here (2023).

The ATLAS Detector

The Large Hadron Collider houses the ATLAS detector. ATLAS is fundamentally a complex Big Data project; designed to observe up to 1.7 billion proton-proton collisions per second, with over 100 million electronic readout channels and a combined data volume of more than 60 million MB/s. The analyses of its data is crucial for advancements in experimental physics.
